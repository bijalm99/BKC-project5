The New Wave of Facial Recognition

Aggregation and analysis of tech company statements on facial recognition, Black Lives Matter protests, and involvement with law enforcement.




  • Next

Within the span of two days, Amazon, Microsoft and IBM took action to either temporarily or permanently cease the development and use of their facial recognition technologies. These decisions were made in response to public outrage about police brutality. It’s been well documented that police are employing mass surveillance tools, particularly facial recognition, to survey protesters and crowds in public spaces.

While some activists focus on the disparate impact of facial recognition, others have called for an outright ban. Facial recognition technology isn’t dangerous just because of its bias. Some activists and scholars have asked whether we should even be striving for perfect facial recognition at all. The use of facial recognition (on drones, for example) to conduct mass surveillance during Black Lives Matter protests, or through neighbourhood watch technology like Amazon Ring, can harm marginalized communities not only through its accuracy rates, but by its very use. Dissenting voices and politics, often rising out of minority groups, have been historically under the watch of law enforcement and constant surveillance.




The Big Players

Click on a company for statements/articles regarding FR, racial injustice, and law enforcement contracts

The first wave of statements from Amazon, Microsoft and IBM appeared as a promising response and an even more promising act of industry self-regulation. It also was a fruitful result of years of organized activism that cautioned against the risks of facial recognition. It has been well-documented that facial recognition technology yields false positives more often for people of colour than white individuals due to biased or unrepresentative data sets. While these companies have pushed against FR use, their continued contracts with military and law enforcement agencies to provide surveillance technologies raise questions about their commitments against racial injustice and discrimination.

The Small Players

While the decisions from larger companies were significant, they do not put a pause on the use of facial recognition in law enforcement activities. Many articles rightly pointed out that these three big players are in fact not the biggest player in the arena of police use of facial recognition. Many smaller companies, like Ayonix, Clearview AI, Cognitec, and NEC, among others, are at the forefront of delivering facial recognition software and capabilities to law enforcement across the United States.
